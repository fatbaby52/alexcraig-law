Wrongful Termination in California

California is an at-will employment state, which means an employer can generally fire an employee for any reason — or no reason at all. But "any reason" does not include illegal reasons. If your employer terminated you in violation of California or federal law, in breach of an employment contract, or in violation of public policy, you may have a wrongful termination claim.

Common categories of unlawful termination include:

  • Discriminatory Firing — Termination motivated by race, color, national origin, ancestry, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, age (40+), disability, medical condition, pregnancy, religion, marital status, military or veteran status, or genetic information.
  • Retaliatory Firing — Termination because you reported harassment or discrimination, filed a workers' compensation claim, requested an accommodation, or otherwise exercised a legal right.
  • Whistleblower Termination — Termination because you reported (or refused to participate in) what you reasonably believed was illegal activity, fraud, or unsafe working conditions. California Labor Code section 1102.5 provides strong whistleblower protections.
  • Termination for Taking Protected Leave — Termination for using FMLA, CFRA, pregnancy disability leave, paid sick leave, kin care, or other protected time off.
  • Constructive Discharge — When working conditions become so intolerable that any reasonable employee would resign. The law treats this as a termination by the employer.
  • Breach of Contract — Termination in violation of the terms of a written or implied employment agreement.
  • Termination in Violation of Public Policy — Such as firing an employee for serving on a jury, voting, or refusing to commit an illegal act at the employer's direction.

Workplace Discrimination

California's Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) is broader and more protective than federal anti-discrimination law. It applies to employers with five or more employees (one or more for harassment claims) and protects a wide range of characteristics. You may have a discrimination claim if you have suffered an adverse employment action — such as termination, demotion, pay cut, denial of promotion, unequal pay, or unjustified discipline — because of:

  • Race, Color, National Origin, or Ancestry
  • Sex, Gender, Gender Identity, or Gender Expression
  • Sexual Orientation
  • Age (40 and Older)
  • Disability or Medical Condition — including the failure to engage in the interactive process or provide a reasonable accommodation
  • Pregnancy, Childbirth, or Related Conditions
  • Religion or Creed — including the failure to accommodate religious practices
  • Marital or Familial Status
  • Military or Veteran Status
  • Genetic Information

Discrimination is rarely announced openly. More often, it is hidden behind pretextual explanations — a sudden performance complaint after years of strong reviews, an unexplained reorganization that targets older workers, or a "culture fit" objection raised only against women or people of color. Building a discrimination case typically requires comparing how the employer treated you with how it treated similarly-situated employees outside your protected class, and identifying inconsistencies in the employer's stated reasons.

Sexual Harassment

No one should have to choose between their dignity and their paycheck. Under FEHA, California recognizes two forms of sexual harassment:

  • Quid Pro Quo Harassment — When a supervisor or someone with authority conditions a job benefit (hiring, promotion, raise, continued employment, favorable assignment) on the employee's submission to sexual advances or other sexual conduct.
  • Hostile Work Environment Harassment — When unwelcome conduct based on sex is sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment. A single severe incident — such as a sexual assault — can be enough to meet this standard.

California law holds employers strictly liable for sexual harassment by supervisors and liable for harassment by coworkers or non-employees where the employer knew or should have known about the harassment and failed to take immediate and appropriate corrective action. Harassment can include unwelcome touching, sexual comments or jokes, sexually explicit images, persistent unwanted advances, threats, and stalking. It can occur in person, by text, by email, or over video — and it is unlawful regardless of the gender of the harasser or the target.

Retaliation

California strongly protects employees who exercise their legal rights. It is unlawful for an employer to retaliate against you — by firing you, demoting you, cutting your pay, disciplining you, or making your working conditions worse — because you engaged in protected activity. Protected activity includes:

  • Reporting discrimination, harassment, or retaliation, either internally or to an outside agency such as the California Civil Rights Department (CRD) or the EEOC
  • Participating in an investigation, hearing, or lawsuit involving an employment law violation
  • Requesting an accommodation for a disability, medical condition, religion, or pregnancy
  • Reporting wage theft or other Labor Code violations to the Labor Commissioner
  • Reporting health and safety violations to Cal/OSHA
  • Filing or testifying about a workers' compensation claim
  • Reporting suspected illegal activity by the employer (whistleblowing)
  • Taking protected leave (FMLA, CFRA, pregnancy disability, sick leave, jury duty)
  • Discussing your wages with coworkers — which is expressly protected by California Labor Code section 232

Wage & Hour Violations

California's wage and hour laws are among the most employee-protective in the nation, but violations are widespread. Many workers do not realize they have a claim until an attorney explains what the law requires. Common violations include:

  • Unpaid Overtime — Non-exempt employees are entitled to time-and-a-half after 8 hours in a day or 40 hours in a week, and double-time after 12 hours in a day. Many employers fail to pay overtime, miscalculate the regular rate, or wrongly classify employees as exempt to avoid paying it.
  • Missed Meal and Rest Breaks — Non-exempt employees are entitled to a 30-minute unpaid, uninterrupted meal break before the end of the 5th hour of work and a paid 10-minute rest break for every 4 hours worked. Missed or interrupted breaks entitle the employee to one hour of premium pay per day.
  • Off-the-Clock Work — Being required or permitted to perform work — answering emails, finishing tasks, opening or closing — without being paid for that time.
  • Independent Contractor Misclassification — Under the ABC test adopted in Dynamex and codified in AB 5, many workers labeled as "contractors" are legally employees and are owed overtime, breaks, expense reimbursement, and other protections.
  • Exempt Misclassification — Salaried employees who do not actually meet the strict duties tests for an executive, administrative, or professional exemption are owed overtime and break premiums.
  • Minimum Wage Violations — California's state minimum wage is higher than the federal minimum, and many cities have local minimum wages that are higher still.
  • Unreimbursed Business Expenses — Under Labor Code section 2802, employers must reimburse necessary work-related expenses, including a reasonable share of personal cell phone and internet use when required for the job.
  • Final Paycheck Violations — Wages are due immediately upon termination, or within 72 hours of a quit without notice. Late final pay triggers "waiting time penalties" of up to 30 days of wages.
  • Inaccurate Wage Statements — Itemized wage statements (pay stubs) must contain specific information required by Labor Code section 226. Defective wage statements can trigger statutory penalties.
  • Tip and Service Charge Violations — Improper tip pooling, withholding tips, or treating mandatory service charges as gratuities.

Severance Agreement Review

Many employers offer departing employees a severance package in exchange for a broad release of legal claims. Before you sign, you need to understand exactly what rights you are giving up — and whether the offered terms are fair. We regularly review and negotiate severance agreements, looking at issues such as:

  • The amount of severance pay relative to the strength of any claims you may have
  • The scope of the release and whether it improperly attempts to release non-waivable rights
  • Non-disparagement, confidentiality, and "no-rehire" clauses (note: California now limits some of these in harassment and discrimination cases)
  • Restrictive covenants — California generally voids non-compete and customer non-solicit clauses
  • Treatment of unvested equity, bonuses, commissions, and benefits
  • References, characterization of the separation, and unemployment eligibility

Severance negotiations are often more productive than people expect, particularly when the employer is aware that the employee may have potential legal claims.

Damages and Remedies Available

Depending on the type of claim and the facts of your case, the remedies available may include:

  • Lost Wages and Benefits — Back pay from the date of the violation, plus front pay where reinstatement is not feasible
  • Emotional Distress Damages — Compensation for the anxiety, depression, humiliation, and disruption caused by the unlawful conduct
  • Punitive Damages — Available in FEHA cases where the employer acted with malice, oppression, or fraud
  • Statutory Penalties — For wage statement violations, waiting time penalties, meal/rest break premiums, and PAGA penalties
  • Liquidated Damages — Available in certain minimum wage and overtime cases
  • Interest — On unpaid wages and other monetary recovery
  • Attorneys' Fees and Costs — Recoverable by prevailing employees in most FEHA, Labor Code, and whistleblower cases
  • Injunctive Relief — Including reinstatement, policy changes, and required training

The Employment Claim Process

  1. Free, Confidential Consultation — We listen to what happened, review any documents you have, and give you an honest assessment of your potential claims.
  2. Evidence Preservation — We help you preserve and gather critical evidence: emails, text messages, performance reviews, pay records, witness names, and timeline notes. Acting quickly matters.
  3. Administrative Filing — For most discrimination, harassment, and retaliation claims, we file a complaint with the California Civil Rights Department (CRD), the EEOC, or the Labor Commissioner. Some claims require this step before filing in court.
  4. Pre-Litigation Demand and Negotiation — Many employment cases resolve before a lawsuit is ever filed. A well-supported demand letter often opens the door to a confidential settlement.
  5. Litigation — If pre-litigation efforts do not produce a fair result, we file suit and pursue the case through discovery, motion practice, mediation, and — when necessary — trial.
  6. Resolution — Most cases ultimately resolve through settlement, but we prepare every matter as if it will go to trial. That preparation is what produces real settlement leverage.

Strict Deadlines Apply

Employment claims are subject to strict and unforgiving deadlines. Under current law, FEHA claims generally must be filed with the California Civil Rights Department within three years of the violation. Most wage claims have a three-year statute of limitations (four years for unfair competition claims). Federal claims under Title VII have a much shorter 180/300-day filing window with the EEOC. Whistleblower retaliation claims under different statutes have their own deadlines, some as short as six months. Evidence — texts, emails, witness memories — also disappears over time. Do not wait. Contact an attorney as soon as you suspect a violation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I really be fired for almost any reason in California?

Yes — and no. California is an at-will employment state, so an employer generally does not need "good cause" to terminate an employee. But "at-will" does not mean "lawless." An employer cannot fire you for a reason that is illegal, such as discrimination, retaliation for protected activity, taking legally protected leave, or in breach of a contract or public policy. If something about your termination feels wrong, it's worth having an attorney evaluate it.

How do I prove discrimination if my employer never said anything obviously biased?

Direct evidence of discrimination — a slur, an admission, a written policy — is rare. Most discrimination cases are proved with circumstantial evidence: comparisons to similarly-situated coworkers outside the protected class, inconsistencies in the employer's stated reasons, suspicious timing, a pattern of treatment over time, and statistical disparities. Part of our job is identifying the evidence already in the employer's records and through discovery.

How long do I have to file an employment claim?

It depends on the type of claim. Most FEHA discrimination, harassment, and retaliation claims must be filed with the California Civil Rights Department within three years. Most wage claims have a three-year statute of limitations under the Labor Code, extended to four years if pursued as an unfair competition claim. Federal Title VII claims have a much shorter EEOC filing deadline. Some whistleblower retaliation statutes have deadlines as short as six months. Do not rely on these general timelines — talk to an attorney as soon as you can to confirm what applies to your situation.

Do I have to pay anything upfront to hire you?

For most employment cases — wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, retaliation, and wage claims — we work on a contingency fee basis. That means there is no upfront cost to you and no attorney's fees unless we recover money for you. Some matters, such as a severance agreement review or contract negotiation, may be handled on a flat fee or hourly basis. We discuss fee arrangements clearly during the free consultation so there are no surprises.

Should I sign the severance agreement my employer offered?

Never sign a severance agreement without having it reviewed by an attorney. Severance agreements typically require you to release a broad range of legal claims — sometimes claims you don't even realize you have. There is often room to negotiate better terms, particularly when potential legal claims are on the table. A short review can pay for itself many times over.

What if I'm still employed — can I bring a claim without getting fired?

Yes. You do not have to lose your job to enforce your rights, and the law protects you against retaliation for raising legitimate complaints. We routinely represent employees who are still on the job. We can also advise you on how to document what's happening, how to make protected complaints, and how to position yourself in case retaliation does occur.

What if I signed an arbitration agreement when I was hired?

Many California employers require employees to sign arbitration agreements as a condition of employment. These agreements are not always enforceable, particularly when they are procedurally or substantively unconscionable, or when they conflict with statutes like the federal Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act. Even when an arbitration agreement is enforceable, you still have the right to pursue your claims — just in a different forum. Bring a copy of any arbitration agreement to your consultation.

Will my employer find out I'm talking to a lawyer?

Your consultation is strictly confidential. Nothing you tell us is shared with your employer without your authorization, and the existence of the consultation itself is privileged. Many employees consult with us long before deciding whether to take any action.
